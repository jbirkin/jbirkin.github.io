Research Interests

Below is a summary of my current research projects:

TEMPLATES and SPT-SMG collaborations

I am currently involved with the JWST Early Release Science Programme TEMPLATES: Targeting Extremely Magnified Panchromatic Lensed Arcs and their Extended Star Formation, an on-going programme designed to spatially resolve the star formation in four galaxies at the peak of cosmic star formation (see the figure below, by Kedar Phadke at the University of Illinois). These galaxies are gravitationally lensed, meaning that they appear much brighter than they do intrinsically, and they can be observed at much higher spatial resolutions. Overall, this allows an incredibly detailed study of the galaxies. My current project involves using NIRSpec observations of two of the four TEMPLATES targets, which are dusty star-forming galaxies selected by the South Pole Telescope (SPT). I am using the Hα and [NII] emission lines to study how the fraction of metals in the interstellar medium (ISM) varies spatially across the source.

You can read the paper, published in ApJ, here.

One of the primary goals of TEMPLATES is to create and share resources for the community, that will assist in working with the plethora of spectacular data which has become available in the early phase of JWST. Our data reduction cookbooks can be found here, and some other useful tools we have developed can be found here.

CRISTAL collaboration

I am also a member of the CRISTAL: [CII] Resolved ISM in Star-Forming Galaxies with ALMA collaboration, an ALMA Cycle-8 Large Program that observed 19 star-forming galaxies at z~4-5 in [CII] line and dust continuum emission. This sample was selected to include galaxies that are representative of the typical population at this epoch, around 1.5 billion years after the Big Bang. Future observations with JWST will also contribute heavily to the study of this sample. The four scientific areas we are aiming to study are shown below. My work is primarily focused on detecting outflows.

Dusty star-forming galaxy kinematics

One of the most pressing questions regarding the dusty star-forming galaxy population is of how their rapid star formation is triggered: are they predominantly merger-driven starbursts? Or can smooth gas accretion from the intergalactic medium (IGM) be sufficient to generate the observed star-formation rates? To address this we study galaxy kinematics, which tells us about the internal motions of the system. We use resolved observations of the Hα emission line to measure galaxy rotational velocities and velocity dispersions, and compare the two to draw conclusions about how dusty star-forming galaxies are supported. In general, we find that they are rapidly rotating systems that also experience high levels of turbulence. This turbulence could be driven by ongoing star formation, infall of cold gas, or mergers and interactions with other galaxies.

This work is part of our ongoing Large Programme with the K-band Multi-Object Spectrograph (KMOS) - known as "KMOS-ALMA Observations of Submillimetre Sources", or "KAOSS" for short. This LP will eventually have involved around 400 hours of KMOS time, and has already produced one of the largest samples of resolved kinematics for high-redshift dusty star-forming galaxies.

You can read the paper here.

Molecular gas in dusty star-forming galaxies

As the fuel for star formation, molecular gas is a highly important component of galaxies to study. For high-redshift galaxies the most commonly used molecular gas tracer is carbon monoxide (CO), which emits at ~mm wavelengths. We recently detected CO emission in 47 DSFGs using ALMA and NOEMA, providing one of the largest samples currently available for this population. The data show that the dusty population typically contains huge reservoirs of molecular gas, which we expect to be consumed on short timescales. We also find evidence to suggest that these galaxies are related to the most massive elliptical galaxies in the local Universe.

You can read the paper, published in MNRAS, here.
